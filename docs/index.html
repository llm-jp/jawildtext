JaWildText: A Benchmark for Vision-Language Models on Japanese Scene Text Understanding

Koki Maeda · Naoaki Okazaki
ICDAR 2026 · arXiv:2603.27942
arXiv PDF GitHub HF Dataset Eval. Detail

3,241 instances · 2,961 images · 1.12M annotated characters · 3,643 character types

Japanese scene text mixes kanji, kana, Latin characters, vertical layouts, handwriting, receipts, signs, and dense public notices. JaWildText turns those conditions into a diagnostic benchmark that separates recognition, reasoning, and formatting failures.

Abstract

JaWildText is a benchmark for evaluating how well vision-language models understand Japanese scene text in real-world images. The benchmark contains 3,241 instances from 2,961 images, with 1.12 million annotated characters across 3,643 unique character types. It combines Dense Scene Text VQA, Receipt Key Information Extraction, and Handwriting OCR so that failures can be traced to recognition, reasoning, or output-formatting errors. Experiments on 14 open-weight VLMs show that even the best model reaches only a 0.64 average score, with kanji recognition emerging as the largest bottleneck.

JaWildText Tasks

Dense STVQA covers text-rich real-world images such as signs, posters, packages, and bulletin boards. Receipt KIE evaluates structured extraction from Japanese receipts. Handwriting OCR tests page-level transcription across paper, whiteboards, and tablets, including horizontal and vertical writing.

Dense STVQA

Open-ended visual question answering over dense Japanese scene text.

Receipt KIE

JSON-style extraction of store, date, total, tax, and line-item fields.

Handwriting OCR

Full-page transcription scored by normalized character error rate.

Dataset Statistics

JaWildText contains 95,705 text regions and covers 1,985 of 2,136 Joyo kanji plus 881 non-Joyo kanji. The subsets stress different mixtures of kanji, kana, digits, Latin characters, short dense notices, long receipts, and free-form handwriting.

Main Results

The main paper evaluates 14 open-weight VLMs with task-specific prompts and scoring: LLM-based binary judging for Dense STVQA, token-level F1 for Receipt KIE, and character-level similarity for Handwriting OCR. Larger models help, but the gap to reliable Japanese wild-text understanding remains substantial.

Error Analysis

Recognition errors dominate Dense STVQA failures, while Handwriting OCR exposes character-level weaknesses for kanji and visually similar scripts. These analyses make JaWildText a diagnostic benchmark rather than only a leaderboard.
